## Selection is context judgment, not abstract comparison

Most technology selection discussions like to build comparison tables, but what actually drives the outcome is rarely the feature checklist. It is the current team's experience, delivery pace, and maintenance model for the next year.

The same stack can have wildly different costs depending on team and business stage. The goal is not to find the "strongest solution" but to judge what fits best right now.

## Early-stage projects should avoid over-engineering

The most common mistake in early projects is introducing abstraction layers and infrastructure for complexity that has not arrived yet. By the time real requirements emerge, the system is already heavy.

A more reliable strategy is to prioritize solutions with low understanding cost and clear replacement paths. Deferring complexity until it is actually needed is almost always more effective than building for imagined scale upfront.

## Good selection criteria include an exit strategy

Technology selection is not just about what you gain by adopting something. It is also about what it costs to leave if it turns out to be wrong. Migration difficulty, ecosystem lock-in, and team learning curve all need to be factored in early.

A stack that can be replaced is usually safer than one that looks the most advanced at first glance — because long-term maintenance needs predictability, not ambition.
